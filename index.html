About Me

I'm passionate about understanding how our world works: how the parts of a system make the whole.

My background is in biogeochemistry: studying how microbial communities and their environments co-evolve over time. I started as a wet-lab researcher, primarily using my computational skills for hobby projects. During the 2020 lockdown, I pivoted to what I really enjoy: processing, wrangling, and visualizing data rather than generating it in the lab.

Since that pivot, I've worked with multidisciplinary datasets from extreme environments (from the deep marine subsurface to acidic mine sites). My work aims to explain how these environments have been shaped by microbial communities and how these communities can drive change in the future.

Ultimately, I simply like to build computational infrastructure and tell data-driven stories.

2. Asgards Around The World.

The Asgardarchaeota are a phylum of archaeal microbes from which we and all other multi-cellular organisms (known as eukaryotes) arose, a process known as Eukaryogenesis. They break our current distinctions between the prokaryotic and eukaryotic worlds by having things are eukaryotic specific proteins (ESP) - which it turns out aren't actually specific to eukaryotes. While the majority of the focus has been on the exciting topic of Eukaryogenesis, it's important to remember that the Asgards are still around! In fact they seem to be everywhere we look -- deep sea sediments, freshwater, salt water, soils (yes, including your backyard!), and in mammal bodies. In this study, my co-authors and I assembled our own Asgard genomes from the deep marine subsurface of Guaymas Basin and did a global survery of all the published Asgard genomes to date to understand what they are doing in the modern. This includes how they contribute to global and local biogeochemical cycles and how they may relevant in remediation and biotechnology.
